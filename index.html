01Messaging Audit

Flex Logs has the right mechanics. The framing still talks mostly to the person configuring it and skips the people who actually approve the spend or carry the audit risk. Three buyers, three different gaps.

"I get what Flex Logs does. I don't know which of my forty log sources actually belong in it."
Datadog's documentation explains what Flex Logs is, not which logs belong there. That decision, query frequency against business criticality, source by source, is left to the customer, so platform teams either over-index out of caution or under-migrate out of inertia. Neither saves money. What this persona needs isn't more pricing explanation, it's a tiering worksheet: a two-axis test (how often is this queried, does it need real-time alerting) that maps a source to Standard, Flex, or Archive without a meeting. Ship it inside the product, not a PDF, and "where logs go" stops being the blocker.
"I don't think in millions of events. I think in what this line item looks like next quarter."
$0.05 per million events is a unit built for the person configuring the tier, not the person approving the budget. By the time log volume shows up on a VP's radar, it's usually because the logs line grew faster than headcount or revenue did, and FinOps practice has caught up to that: most teams managing SaaS spend now treat platform bills like this one as an active line item, not something engineering quietly absorbs. The rewrite for this buyer isn't a cheaper number, it's a different sentence: Flex Logs is the lever that lets log volume grow without the bill growing at the same rate. That's a forecasting claim, not a pricing detail, and it's the one this buyer actually needs to repeat upward to their own leadership.
"Cost isn't my problem. Producing this log in the next ten minutes is."
Flex Frozen and Archive Search were built for exactly this person, long retention with full searchability and no rehydration step, but they still get introduced as cost or storage features. For a compliance or security buyer, cheaper storage is a nice side effect of the real value: when an examiner, auditor, or incident responder asks for a log from fourteen months ago, it's already searchable, not buried in an archive that needs re-indexing under deadline. That's an audit-readiness story, and it deserves to be told as one, separately from the cost narrative aimed at engineering leadership in the tab above.
02Competitive Intelligence

Three different conversations get lumped into one "why not just use something cheaper" objection on a call. They need three different answers, not one deflection.

Objection source
Grafana Loki
Heard on the call

"We could run Loki ourselves and pay a fraction of what Datadog charges per GB."

Why customers believe this

Loki's label-only indexing is genuinely cheaper, and Grafana Cloud's published per-GB pricing undercuts Datadog's ingestion rate on paper.

The answer

Flex Logs makes the same trade: index a subset, store the rest cheaply, inside the platform that already correlates the log with the trace and the host metric. Nothing new to operate.

Where the gap is real

If a team already built capacity around Loki, Mimir, and Tempo, the conversation isn't price anymore, it's sunk cost and switching risk. No pricing page wins that one.

Objection source
Splunk
Heard on the call

"Splunk's SPL does more for our security investigations than Datadog's log search."

Why customers believe this

Splunk's query depth and SIEM maturity are real strengths, and it's still the default name in security, even as cost is what pushes teams to look elsewhere.

The answer

Flex Frozen and Archive Search target teams outgrowing Splunk's retention costs: long-term searchable storage without the rehydration tax that makes Splunk's cold tier painful to use.

Where the gap is real

Full SPL-level parity for the deepest security workflows isn't there yet. That's a roadmap conversation, not one messaging should paper over.

Objection source
Pipeline tools
Heard on the call

"We don't need to switch platforms, we just filter logs before they hit Datadog and the bill drops."

Why customers believe this

This is a legitimate, fast-growing motion (Cribl, Edge Delta, and similar), and the publicly quoted savings, often 50 to 80 percent, are too large to wave off.

The answer

Pipeline filtering and Flex Logs aren't competing claims. One decides what to send, the other decides where to put it. The strongest version treats pipeline vendors as an on-ramp into Flex, not a threat.

Where the gap is real

If Flex Logs keeps getting messaged as purely defensive, the "we help you control this" narrative gets ceded to the pipeline vendors. That's a messaging fix, not a product one.

03GTM Brief
Core thesis

Flex Logs solved the cost objection in 2023. Federated Logs and BYOC Logs, both announced at DASH 2026, solve the next one: where the data already lives.

Right now those read as three separate launches. The GTM job is making them read as one continuing argument about log economics, not three announcements that fade into their own roundup post. The window to do it is short: Federated Logs and BYOC Logs shipped two and a half weeks ago, while customers and press are still paying attention.

ICP priority

Two buyers worth prioritizing: AI-scale engineering orgs, and regulated orgs needing data residency

Agent framework adoption nearly doubled year over year heading into 2026, and services running on those frameworks more than doubled in the same window. That's already showing up in telemetry Datadog itself collects, an unusually well-timed moment to connect Flex Logs to a growth driver the company already measures.

BYOC Logs runs Datadog's log engine inside the customer's own infrastructure, answering a buyer once told that keeping logs in-region meant leaving the platform. Pair it with the Part 01 compliance story, not a feature roundup line item.

Channel priority

Content plus a public version of the cost framework

Own the "Splunk alternative," "Loki vs Datadog," and "Datadog logs cost" search intent, and give the visitor a working volume calculator, the same model running in this page's hero, instead of a static pricing table. The value should be felt before a sales conversation starts.

05Sales Enablement

One battlecard moment, written the way it would actually get used on a call, plus two compact ones for quick reference.

"Our logs bill is already too high. Why would we add more volume to anything, even Flex?"
Talk trackFlex isn't more volume on the expensive meter, it's the same data moved to the cheap one. Walk me through your indexed sources for a minute, I'd bet a third haven't been queried in 30 days. Those are Flex candidates today, not someday.
Bring to the callThe Part 01 tiering worksheet, used live on the call, not emailed afterward.
LandmineDon't lead with the $0.05 number. It invites the prospect to do napkin math against their full indexed volume, which isn't the real comparison. Lead with "what's not being queried" instead.
"We'll just filter with Cribl before it hits Datadog."
One-linerGood instinct, different layer. Pipeline tools decide what gets sent. Flex decides where the sent data lives. Most teams running both save more than either alone.
"We're worried about losing visibility if logs move to Flex."
One-linerFair concern: Flex doesn't support real-time monitors today. That's exactly why it's for sources you're not alerting on, anything that needs alerting stays in Standard. Per-source, not all-or-nothing.
When not to push it

Every card above defends Flex. A rep who knows when to hold back earns more trust on the next call than one who doesn't.

  • The source needs real-time monitors or alerting. Flex doesn't support that today, pushing it here breaks something the customer relies on.
  • Volume is small enough that Standard Indexing already sits inside budget. Flex adds tiering overhead for savings that won't show up on the bill.
  • It's live incident response on the last few hours of data. Flex's compute model is built for occasional, ad-hoc queries, not active firefighting.
